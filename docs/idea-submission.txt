heirloom - a dead man's switch for Stellar accounts

Sign one transaction today. If your account goes quiet for a period you choose,
a recipient you named takes it over. Your assets never leave your account and no
contract ever holds them.


PROBLEM

Self-custody has one unsolved failure mode: the key stops being available. The
owner dies, or - far more often - simply loses access. This started personally:
I lose wallets, repeatedly, and on-chain there is no lost-and-found.

Existing answers all cost you something. Give a company your keys and you are no
longer self-custodial. Give a person your seed phrase and they can rob you
tonight. Deposit into a vault contract and your assets leave your control for
code you have to trust.

The recipient does not have to be someone else. It can be a fresh wallet you
own: lose your keys, do nothing, and the account walks itself over to your new
wallet. Inheritance is the emotional case; recovering from your own carelessness
is the everyday one.


WHY STELLAR

CAP-21 preconditions, specifically minSeqAge: "valid only once this account has
been untouched for N seconds." That turns a dead man's switch from an
application into a network rule.

On Ethereum a dead man's switch means depositing into a contract and trusting
its code. Here the assets never move, so there is nothing to drain. The
recipient holds a signed transaction that validators reject until the silence
condition is met - so handing it to them costs nothing.

minSeqAge is one of the least-used features in the protocol. I verified it on
testnet before writing this rather than trusting the docs: 10 of 10 checks pass,
including a real takeover (tx 1be159fb0867dbc8c0b73c3f5bd813e2d7b3c8eca20a1992d055c416d039c82f).


TWO MODES

The protocol supports two products, and the only difference is whether
minSeqNum is set.

Standing plan - stays armed indefinitely; ordinary activity only restarts the
idle clock. Verified: 3 transactions, then silence, and the takeover succeeded.
Cancelled deliberately, by bumping the sequence past the reserved slot.

Sealed plan - one-shot; any transaction destroys it permanently. Verified: a
single transaction and the takeover was rejected forever.

Users must choose consciously. Standing is convenient but must be actively
cancelled. Sealed is safer against a stale recipient but dies silently the
moment you use your account.


TARGET USERS

Self-custody holders who have already lost a wallet once - the wedge, because
the pitch needs no discussion of death, only "what happens if you lose your
phone?" Then long-term holders and families unwilling to hand a seed phrase to a
relative, and small teams needing continuity when a signer disappears.


TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE

Frontend (Next.js): setup wizard, dashboard with idle countdown and an "I'm
here" heartbeat, recipient view.

Stellar layer, no custody: the takeover is a SetOptions transaction making the
recipient a signer and setting master weight to 0, carrying minSeqAge (plus
minSeqNum for standing plans). The heartbeat is any transaction. Cancelling is a
BumpSequence past the reserved sequence slot.

Soroban registry contract: plan metadata, recipient discovery, public countdown.
It exists for discoverability, not custody - destroy it and every plan still
works, because the recipient only needs their XDR.

The signed XDR is not a secret. There is no server holding anything that could
be leaked or coerced into releasing it early; the network does the refusing.


COMPLEXITY

Almost no production code uses minSeqAge, so correctness has to be proven
against a live network. That is how I found that a brand-new account has
seqTime = 0 and is treated as infinitely idle, letting a recipient claim
instantly - so arming a plan must include an on-chain transaction.

Other hard parts: pre-signed fees are fixed years in advance (fee-bump at claim
time), recipients may hold zero XLM (fee sponsorship), cancellation must reach
the reserved sequence number rather than merely exceed minSeqNum, and master
weight 0 is irreversible.


ROADMAP

MVP on testnet: wallet, heartbeat and countdown, then the registry contract,
then the full loop with both modes and CI running the proof on every commit.

Acquisition: the story is the pitch. Starting with the people around me and
Stellar community channels, with a demo that compresses the period to minutes so
arming, heartbeat, a rejected early claim and a successful claim all fit in
under two minutes.

Mainnet: security review of the arming and cancellation paths first, since those
are where a bug costs someone their account. Then conservative defaults, fee
sponsorship so recipients never need funding, and multi-recipient splits.


OUT OF SCOPE

Death certificates, oracles, identity verification - the whole point is needing
none. v1 also excludes percentage splits, legal will integration and KYC.
